What We Are Doing Today

Today we are going to build a prediction equation together using real housing data that you collected in class.

We are not calculating regression coefficients yet.
We are learning what a regression equation is trying to say.

By the end of this exercise, you should understand:

  • What a predicted value (\(\hat{y}\)) represents
  • How an equation encodes a prediction rule
  • Why predictions can be wrong even when they seem reasonable

Step 3: What Are We Predicting?

Our goal for today is to predict:

House price

Using only:

Square footage

We are pretending to be a national housing price prediction system.

It is obvious that larger homes tend to cost more. Our goal is not to state that fact, but to be precise about it.

Specifically, we want to do two things:

  1. Quantify the change in cost (\(y\)) associated with each increase in square footage (\(x\)).
    In other words, as a home gets larger, how much more expensive do we expect it to be?

  2. Use that relationship to make predictions. Given a specific square footage, we want the model to produce a predicted home price, particularlyn for homes we have not directly observed.

This is what a simple linear regression model is designed to do.

Important constraint:

We are allowed to use only square footage.
We are not allowed to use state, neighborhood, or any other information.

Step 4: Thinking in Terms of Change (Not Ratios)

We are not asking:

  • “What is the price per square foot?”

Instead, we are asking:

“As square footage increases, how much does price tend to increase?”

This is a question about change, not division.

Step 5: The Class Prediction Rule (Verbal)

As a class, we agree on one rule that applies to all homes:

For every additional 100 square feet of house size, the predicted price increases by approximately $XXXX.

The value $XXXX is chosen by the class based on the pooled data.

Why do we talk about 100 square feet instead of 1 square foot?

In theory, we could describe the change in price for every single additional square foot.
In practice, that change would be very small and hard to interpret.

Housing prices typically change by thousands of dollars, not by a few dollars at a time.
If we used 1 square foot as the unit, the number would be tiny and not very meaningful on its own.

Using 100 square feet:

  • Matches the scale at which people think about home size (rooms, additions, renovations)
  • Produces numbers that are easier to read, compare, and discuss
  • Makes it clearer how changes in size translate into noticeable changes in price

This choice does not change the relationship between size and price.
It only changes how we describe that relationship in words.

NoteWhy we do not use 1 square foot (and why this matters for storytelling)

By default, a regression model calculates change for a 1-unit increase in the predictor.
In this case, that means the model naturally estimates the change in price for 1 additional square foot.

There is nothing mathematically wrong with that.

However, 1 square foot is not a meaningful unit for telling a housing story.

For example, suppose the model estimates that price increases by:

$2.50 for every additional square foot

That is a correct statement—but it sounds trivial.

Now tell the same story using a more meaningful unit:

  • $2.50 per 1 sq ft
  • $250 per 100 sq ft
  • $2,500 per 1,000 sq ft

All three describe the same relationship.
None of them change the model.

What changes is how clearly the story communicates real-world impact.

This is why we choose to talk about 100 square feet:

  • It reflects how people actually think about housing size
  • It turns a tiny coefficient into a meaningful narrative
  • It makes the effect of size on price easier to interpret and discuss

Choosing the unit is not changing the model—it is choosing how to tell the story of the model.

Step 5.5: Converting the Default 1-Unit Change into a 100-Unit Change

Regression models are built around a simple idea: a coefficient describes the change in predicted \(y\) for a 1-unit increase in \(x\).

By default, a regression model estimates the change in price for a 1-unit increase in square footage.

That means the model first produces a number that represents:

How much the predicted price changes for one additional square foot.

We label that number using a symbol, called the coefficient for square footage, written as \(b_1\).

For example, if the model estimates:

\[ b_1 = 2.50 \]

then the story is:

For every additional 1 square foot, the predicted price increases by $2.50.

But we usually want a more meaningful story unit—like 100 square feet.

Converting from 1 sq ft to 100 sq ft

A 100 sq ft increase is just 100 one-sq-ft increases. So:

\[ \text{Change for 100 sq ft} = 100 \times (\text{change for 1 sq ft}) \]

That means:

\[ \text{Dollars per 100 sq ft} = 100 \times b_1 \]

Example (so you can see the difference in the story)

Suppose a model gives:

\[ b_1 = 2.50 \]

This means:

  • Per 1 sq ft: predicted price increases by $2.50
  • Per 100 sq ft: predicted price increases by:

\[ 100 \times 2.50 = 250 \]

So the same relationship can be told as:

  • “For every additional 1 sq ft, price increases by $2.50.”
  • “For every additional 100 sq ft, price increases by $250.”

These statements are mathematically identical, but the second one is usually a clearer real-world story.

How we are using this in class today

Today, the class will choose $XXXX per 100 sq ft directly because it is a meaningful unit for housing.

If we wanted to express the same rule as a “default” 1 sq ft change, we would divide by 100:

\[ \text{Dollars per 1 sq ft} = \frac{\text{XXXX}}{100} \]

So:

  • \(XXXX\) per 100 sq ft is the same as \(\frac{XXXX}{100}\) per 1 sq ft.

We are not changing the model—we are choosing a unit that helps us interpret and communicate the relationship clearly.

Step 6: Writing the Prediction Equation

Now we translate the verbal rule into an equation.

This equation does not describe what actually happened.
It describes how our model makes predictions.

The prediction equation has the form:

\[ \hat{y} = b_0 + b_1(\text{square footage}) \]

Where:

  • \(\hat{y}\) = predicted house price
  • \(b_1\) = how much price changes as square footage increases
  • \(b_0\) = a baseline price

Using the class-chosen value, the equation becomes:

\[ \hat{y} = b_0 + (\text{XXXX per 100 sq ft}) \times \text{square footage} \]

This is our equation.

Step 7: Predicting a House We Do Not Observe

Now we use the equation to predict the price of a house that does not appear in the dataset.

Prediction Question

If a house is exactly 1,725 square feet, what price does our equation predict?

Using the class equation:

\[ \hat{y}_{1725} = b_0 + (\text{XXXX per 100 sq ft}) \times 1725 \]

This predicted value is written as \(\hat{y}\).

\(\hat{y}\) is the model’s guess for a house size we have never observed.

Step 8: Comparing Predictions to Reality

For selected homes in the dataset, we:

  1. Plug their square footage into the equation
  2. Compute a predicted price (\(\hat{y}\))
  3. Compare it to the actual price (\(y\))

Prediction error is defined as:

\[ \text{Error} = y - \hat{y} \]

Interpretation:

  • Positive error → the model under-valued the home
  • Negative error → the model over-valued the home

Step 9: Diagnosing the Model

As a class, we ask:

  • Are errors small or large?
  • Are they random?
  • Do errors appear to cluster by state?

We do not fix the model yet.

What You Should Take Away from Today

By the end of today, you should understand that:

  • A regression equation is a prediction rule
  • \(\hat{y}\) is a model-based guess
  • Coefficients describe change, not ratios
  • Error reveals missing structure, not failure

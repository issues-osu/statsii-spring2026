Simple Linear Regression: SAT Math predicted by SAT Verbal

Author

Barboza-Salerno

Published

February 1, 2026

Introduction

In this analysis, we will explore the relationship between SAT Verbal scores (Independent Variable) and SAT Math scores (Dependent Variable). We aim to build a simple linear regression model to predict math scores based on verbal scores.

Data Loading

First, we load the necessary libraries and the dataset.

SLR Mechanics

Here we walk through the calculations, model fitting, and interpretation.

Correlation Analysis

We calculate the Pearson Correlation Coefficient (\(r\)) to quantify the strength and direction of the linear relationship.

cor_test <- cor.test(df_analysis$SAT_verbal, df_analysis$SAT_math)
r_value <- cor_test$estimate
p_value <- cor_test$p.value

Interpretation: The correlation is \(r = 0.464\). *

Magnitude: This indicates a moderate-to-strong relationship. *

Direction: The positive sign indicates that as Verbal scores increase, Math scores also tend to increase.

Simple Linear Regression Model

We fit the following linear model:

\[ \text{SAT\_math} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{SAT\_verbal} + \epsilon \]

# Fit the model
model <- lm(SAT_math ~ SAT_verbal, data = df_analysis)

Interpretation of Coefficients

Let’s break down what these numbers mean:

  1. Intercept (\(\beta_0\)):
    • Value: 328.66
    • Interpretation: This is the predicted SAT Math score for a student who scored 0 on SAT Verbal.
    • Note: Since a score of 0 is effectively impossible/outside the normal range, this value serves mostly as a mathematical anchor for the regression line.
  2. Slope (\(\beta_1\) - SAT Verbal):
    • Value: 0.46
    • Interpretation: For every 1 point increase in SAT Verbal score, we expect the SAT Math score to increase by approximately 0.46 points.
    • This quantifies the “exchange rate” between verbal and math skills in this dataset.

Model Performance

  • R-Squared (\(R^2\)): 0.215
    • Meaning: Approximately 21.5% of the variance in SAT Math scores can be explained by SAT Verbal scores. The remaining variance is due to other factors not in our model.
  • Residual Standard Error (Sigma/MSE): 60.82
    • Meaning: On average, our model’s predictions are off by about 60.82 points. This represents the average “miss” distance of our regression line.

Hypothesis Testing Explained

In regression, we test the following hypotheses about the slope (\(\beta_1\)):

  • Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)): \(\beta_1 = 0\)
    • Translation: There is NO relationship between Verbal and Math scores. Changing the Verbal score does not help predict the Math score. The slope of the line is flat.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_1\)): \(\beta_1 \neq 0\)
    • Translation: There IS a relationship. Knowing the Verbal score does give us information about the Math score. The slope is not flat.

Interpreting the P-Value

  • The p-value for our SAT Verbal coefficient is < 0.001.
  • Decision: Since \(p < 0.05\) (our standard threshold for significance), we reject the Null Hypothesis.
  • Conclusion: We have statistically significant evidence that SAT Verbal scores are a predictor of SAT Math scores. The relationship we see in the data is unlikely to be due to random chance.

Simple Linear Regression: Understanding Relationships Between Variables

A foundation for statistical modeling

Slide 2: What is Simple Linear Regression (SLR)?

  • title: “What is Simple Linear Regression?”

  • content: | Definition:
    Simple Linear Regression is a statistical method used to model and analyze the relationship between two variables: one dependent (response) variable and one independent (predictor) variable.

    Key Idea:
    SLR estimates a linear equation (Ž̂ = β0 + β1X) that predicts the dependent variable (Ž) based on the independent variable (X).

  • visual: “Diagram illustrating dependent and independent variables.”

Slide 3: What is SLR Useful For?

  • title: “What is SLR Useful For?”
  • content: | Applications:
    • Predicting outcomes (e.g., predicting sales from advertising spend).
    • Understanding relationships (e.g., relationship between study hours and test scores).
    • Quantifying the impact of one variable on another.
    Why It Matters:
    SLR is foundational for more complex modeling techniques.

  • visual: “Scatterplots with strong, weak, and no relationships.”

Slide 4: Types of Data Needed

  • title: “Types of Data Needed”
  • content: | Requirements for Data:
    • Dependent Variable (Ž): Continuous (e.g., height, income).
    • Independent Variable (X): Can be continuous or categorical (e.g., age, treatment group).
    Note: Assumptions (e.g., normality, homoscedasticity) will be covered in another lecture.

  • visual: “Table of dependent and independent variable examples.”

Slide 5: Statistical and Conceptual Model

  • title: “Statistical and Conceptual Model”
  • content: | Statistical Model:
    Ž = β0 + β1X + ε
    • β0 (Intercept): Predicted value of Ž when X = 0.
    • β1 (Slope): Change in Ž for a one-unit change in X.
    • ε (Error Term): Represents variability not explained by X.
    Conceptual Model:
    Focuses on understanding the direction and strength of the relationship.

  • visual: “Equation labeled on scatterplot.”

Slide 6: Interpreting the Coefficients

  • title: “Interpreting the Coefficients”

  • content: | Intercept (β0): The expected value of Ž when X = 0.

    Slope (β1): For each one-unit increase in X, Ž changes by β1 units.

  • visual: “Annotated scatterplot highlighting slope and intercept.”

Slide 7: Examples

  • title: “Examples”
  • content: | Example 1: Predicting test scores from study hours.
    • β0: Predicted score when study hours = 0.
    • β1: Increase in score per additional hour.
    Example 2: Estimating house prices from square footage.
    • β0: Predicted price when square footage = 0.
    • β1: Increase in price per square foot.

  • visual: “Side-by-side scatterplots for examples.”

Slide 8: Mean Centering

  • title: “Mean Centering”

  • content: | What is Mean Centering? Subtracting the mean of X from each value to create a centered variable.

    Why Do It?

    • Changes the intercept to represent Ž at average X.
    • Does not change slope or model fit.

    Comparison:

    • Without Centering: Intercept at X = 0.
    • With Centering: Intercept at mean(X).

  • visual: “Two scatterplots with and without mean centering.”

Slide 9: How to Perform SLR in R

  • title: “How to Perform SLR in R”
  • content: | R Packages:
    • lm() for modeling.
    • ggplot2 for visualization.
    • car for diagnostics.
    Example Code:
    R library(ggplot2) data <- data.frame(X = 1:5, Y = c(2, 4, 6, 8, 10)) model <- lm(Y ~ X, data) summary(model) ggplot(data, aes(X, Y)) + geom_point() + geom_smooth(method = "lm")
